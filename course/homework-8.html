    Foundations of Mathematics
    🔗
    Here are some attempted proofs, adapted by a colleague from past FOM student answers, of the following statement:
    🔗
    Suppose \(n\) is a positive integer. If \(n^2\) is a multiple of \(3\text{,}\) then \(n\) is a multiple of \(3\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    For each of the following proofs:
    🔗
    • Read through the proof line by line. Does each line follow from a known mathematical fact or from previous lines?
      🔗
      🔗
    • Decide whether it is a valid proof. Why do you think it is valid or invalid?
      🔗
      🔗
    • Grade the proof on a scale from \(1\) (does not verify the statement at all) to \(5\) (a completely clear, convincing, and logically sound argument).
      🔗
      🔗
    • What advice would you give the proof writer on how to change the proof?
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    1. Let \(n\) be an integer such that \(n^2=3x\) where \(x\) is an integer. Then \(3 | n^2\text{.}\) Since \(n^2 = 3x\text{,}\) \(nn = 3x\text{.}\) Thus \(3 | n\text{.}\) Therefore if \(n^2\) is a multiple of 3, then \(n\) is a multiple of 3.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Assume that \(n^2\) is an odd positive integer that is divisible by 3. That is \(n^2 = (3n+1)^2 = 9n^2 + 6n +1 = 3n(n+2)+1\text{.}\) Therefore, \(n^2\) is divisible by 3. Assume that \(n^2\) is even and a multiple of 3. That is, \(n^2 = (3n)^2 = 9n^2 = 3n(3n)\text{.}\) Therefore, \(n^2\) is a multiple of 3. If we factor \(n^2 = 9n^2\text{,}\) we get \(3n(3n)\text{,}\) which means that \(n\) is a multiple of 3.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Let \(n\) be a positive integer such that \(n^2\) is a multiple of 3. Then \(n=3m\) where \(m \in {\bf Z}\text{.}\) So \(n^2 = (3m)^2 = 9 m^2 = 3 (3m^2).\) This breaks down into \(3m\) times \(3m\) which shows that \(m\) is a multiple of 3.
      🔗
      🔗
    4. Suppose \(n\) is not a multiple of 3. We will let \(3k\) be a positive integer that is a multiple of 3, so that \(3k+1\) and \(3k+2\) are integers that are not multiples of 3. Now \(n^2 = (3k+1)^2 = 9k^2 + 6k +1 = 3(3k^2+2k) + 1\text{.}\) Since \(3(3k^2+2k)\) is a multiple of 3, \(3(3k^2+2k)+1\) is not. For the other case, \(n^2 = (3k+2)^2 = 9k^2 + 12k +4 = 3(3k^2+4k+1) + 1\text{,}\) which is not a multiple of 3.
      🔗
      🔗
